6  Geometric Brownian Motion

An important Ito process is geometric Brownian motion given by \[ S_t=S_0\mathrm{e}^{(\mu- \sigma^2 /2)t + \sigma B_t} \qquad(6.1)\]

for constants \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\), where \(B\) is a Brownian motion. Using the product rule and the rule for exponentials, we obtain \[ \frac{\,\mathrm{d}S}{S} = \mu\,\mathrm{d}t+\sigma\,\mathrm{d}B\;. \qquad(6.2)\]

When we see an equation of the form Equation 6.2, we should recognize Equation 6.1 as the solution.

The process \(S\) is called a geometric Brownian motion. In keeping with the discussion of Section 5.2, we interpret Equation 6.2 as stating that \(\mu\,\mathrm{d}t\) is the expected rate of change of \(S\) and \(\sigma^2\,\mathrm{d}t\) is the variance of the rate of change in an instant \(\,\mathrm{d}t\). We call \(\mu\) the drift and \(\sigma\) the volatility.

Taking the natural logarithm of Equation 6.1 gives an equivalent form of the solution: \[ \log S_t= \log S_0+\left(\mu -\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)t + \sigma B_t\;. \qquad(6.3)\] This shows that \(\log S_t - \log S_0\) is a \((\mu-\sigma^2/2,\sigma)\)–Brownian motion. Given information at time \(t\), the logarithm of \(S(u)\) for \(u>t\) is normally distributed with mean \((u-t)(\mu-\sigma^2/2)\) and variance \((u-t)\sigma^2\). Because \(S\) is the exponential of its logarithm, \(S\) can never be negative. For this reason, a geometric Brownian motion is a better model for stock prices than is a Brownian motion.

The differential of Equation 6.3 is \[ \,\mathrm{d}\log S_t = \left(\mu -\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)\,\mathrm{d}t+ \sigma\,\mathrm{d}B_t\;. \qquad(6.4)\]

We conclude:

Calculation Rule 6.1
The equation \[\frac{\,\mathrm{d}S}{S} = \mu\,\mathrm{d}t+\sigma\,\mathrm{d}B\] is equivalent to the equation \[\,\mathrm{d}\log S = \left(\mu -\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)\,\mathrm{d}t+ \sigma\,\mathrm{d}B\; .\] The solution of both equations is Equation 6.1 or the equivalent Equation 6.3.

In what follows, we describe some important properties of geometric Brownian motions. The last two sections of the chapter address processes satisfying Equation 6.2 when \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) are not constant.

6.1 Lognormal Variables and Average Growth Rates

A random variable is said to be lognormally distributed if it can be written as \(\tilde{y}= \mathrm{e}^{\tilde{x}}\) where \(\tilde{x}\) has a normal distribution with mean \(m\) and standard deviation \(s\). The mean of a lognormal random variable is given by the following.

Calculation Rule 6.2
If \(\tilde{x}\) is normally distributed with mean \(m\) and standard deviation \(s\), then \(\mathrm{e}^{\tilde{x}}\) is lognormally distributed and \[\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{e}^{\tilde{x}}]=e^{m + \frac{1}{2} s^2}\,. \qquad(6.5)\]

Equation 6.1 shows that if \(S\) is a geometric Brownian motion, then, for each \(t<u\), \[\frac{S_u}{S_t} = \mathrm{e}^{(\mu-\sigma^2/2)(u-t) + \sigma(B_u-B_t)}\,,\] which is a lognormally distributed random variable, conditional on information at \(t\). We can apply the previous calculation rule by taking \(m=(\mu-\sigma^2/2)(u-t)\) and \(s=\sigma\sqrt{u-t}\). This produces \[\mathbb{E}_t[S_u] = S_t\mathrm{e}^{(u-t)\mu}\,.\] Thus, a geometric Brownian motion grows at the average rate of \(\mu\).

A special case is \(\mu=0\). When \(\mu=0\), we have \(\mathbb{E}_t[S_u]=S_t\), so \(S\) is a martingale. This is an important example of a strictly positive martingale.

6.2 Distribution of Continuously Compounded Returns

Over a discrete time interval \(\Delta t\), Equation 6.4 implies that the change in the logarithm of \(S\) is \[ \Delta \log S = \left(\mu -\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)\Delta t+ \sigma\,\Delta B\;. \qquad(6.6)\]

If \(S\) is the price of a dividend-reinvested asset, then over the time period \(t_{i-1}\) to \(t_i\), with \(t_i-t_{i-1}=\Delta t\), we have \[ \Delta \log S = r_i\,\Delta t\;, \qquad(6.7)\]

where \(r_i\) is the continuously compounded annualized rate of return during the period \(\Delta t\). This follows from the definition of the continuously compounded rate of return as the constant rate over the time period \(\Delta t\) that would cause \(S\) to grow (or fall) from \(S_{t_{i-1}}\) to \(S_{t_i}\). To be precise, \(r_i\) is defined by \[\frac{S_{t_i}}{S_{t_{i-1}}} = \mathrm{e}^{r_i\Delta t}\; ,\] which is equivalent to Equation 6.7. Thus, when a dividend-reinvested asset price is a geometric Brownian motions satisfying Equation 6.2, then the continuously compounded annualized rate of return over a period of length \(\Delta t\) is \[r_i = \mu -\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2+ \frac{\sigma\Delta B}{\Delta t}\; .\] This implies that \(r_i\) is normally distributed with mean \(\mu-\sigma^2/2\) and variance \(\sigma^2/\Delta t\). Given historical data on the rates of return, the parameters \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) can be estimated by standard methods (see Chapter 16).

6.3 Tail Probabilities of Geometric Brownian Motions

An important calculation for pricing options is to compute the probability of an option finishing in the money under different probability measures. Consider computing the probability at date \(0<t\) of the event that \(S_t > K\) for some positive constant \(K\), where \(S\) is a geometric Brownian motion satisfying Equation 6.2. We use the logarithmic version of the solution given in Equation 6.3. We have \[\begin{align*} S_t > K & \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \log S_t > \log K\\ & \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \sigma B_t > \log K - \log S_0-\mu t \\ & \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \frac{B_t}{\sqrt{t}} > \frac{\log K - \log S_0-\mu t}{\sigma\sqrt{t}} \\ & \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad -\frac{B_t}{\sqrt{t}} < \frac{\log S_0-\log K + \mu t}{\sigma\sqrt{t}} \end{align*} \qquad(6.8)\] We denote the probability that a standard normal random variable is less than some number \(d\) as \(\mathrm{N}(d)\). Because \(-B_t / \sqrt{t}\) is a standard normal random variable, the above calculations imply the following.

Calculation Rule 6.3
Assume \(S\) is a geometric Brownian motion satisfying Equation 6.2$. Then, for any positive number \(K\) \[ \text{prob}(S_t>K) = \mathrm{N}(d)\;, \qquad(6.9)\]

where \[ d = \frac{\log S_0 - \log K+ \mu t}{\sigma\sqrt{t}}\;. \qquad(6.10)\] Furthermore, \[ \text{prob}(S_t<K) = 1 - \mathrm{N}(d) = \mathrm{N}(-d)\;. \qquad(6.11)\]

6.4 Non-Constant Coefficients

If \(\,\mathrm{d}S/S = \mu \,\mathrm{d}t + \sigma \,\mathrm{d}B\) but \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) are not constants, then \(S\) is not called a geometric Brownian motion. However, we can solve this equation using arguments similar to those used at the beginning of this chapter. We obtain \[S_t = S_0 \exp\left(\int_0^t (\mu_s - \sigma_s^2/2)\,\,\mathrm{d}s + \int_0^t \sigma_s\,\mathrm{d}B_s\right)\,,\] and \[\log S_t = \log S_0 + \int_0^t (\mu_s - \sigma_s^2/2)\,\,\mathrm{d}s + \int_0^t \sigma_s\,\mathrm{d}B_s\,.\]

6.5 Volatilities of Products and Ratios

We sometimes need to know the volatility of an Ito process \(S\) formed as a ratio or product of two Ito processes \(S_i\), given that \[\frac{\,\mathrm{d}S_i}{S_i} = \mu_i\,\mathrm{d}t + \sigma \,\mathrm{d}B_i\] for Brownian motions \(B_1\) and \(B_2\). Here, we allow \(\mu_i\) and \(\sigma_i\) to be stochastic processes, so the \(S_i\) are not necessarily geometric Brownian motions. Let \(\rho\) denote the correlation process of the Brownian motions \(B_1\) and \(B_2\).
As mentioned in Chapter 6, when we encounter an equation of the form \[\frac{\,\mathrm{d}S}{S} = \mu\,\mathrm{d}t + \sigma\,\mathrm{d}B\] where \(B\) is a Brownian motion, we will say \(\sigma\) is the volatility of \(S\). We will occasionally need to compute the volatilities of products or ratios of random processes. These computations follow directly from Ito’s formula.

Suppose \[\frac{\,\mathrm{d}X}{X} = \mu_x\,\mathrm{d}t + \sigma_x\,\mathrm{d}B_x \qquad \text{and} \qquad \frac{\,\mathrm{d}Y}{Y} = \mu_y\,\mathrm{d}t + \sigma_y\,\mathrm{d}B_y\; ,\] where \(B_x\) and \(B_y\) are Brownian motions with correlation \(\rho\), and \(\mu_x\), \(\mu_y\), \(\sigma_x\), \(\sigma_y\), and \(\rho\) may be quite general random processes.

Products

If \(Z=XY\), then Equation 5.28 gives us \[ \frac{\,\mathrm{d}Z}{Z} = (\mu_x+\mu_y+\rho\sigma_x\sigma_y)\,\mathrm{d}t + \sigma_x\,\mathrm{d}B_x + \sigma_y\,\mathrm{d}B_y\;. \qquad(6.12)\]

The instantaneous variance of \(\,\mathrm{d}Z/Z\) is calculated, using the rules for products of differentials, as \[\begin{align*} \left(\frac{\,\mathrm{d}Z}{Z}\right)^2 &= (\sigma_x\,\mathrm{d}B_x + \sigma_y\,\mathrm{d}B_y)^2\\ &= (\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_y^2 + 2\rho\sigma_x\sigma_y)\,\mathrm{d}t\;. \end{align*}\] As will be explained below, the volatility is the square root of the instantaneous variance (dropping the \(\,\mathrm{d}t\)). This implies:

Calculation Rule 6.4
The volatility of \(XY\) is \[ \sqrt{\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_y^2 + 2\rho\sigma_x\sigma_y}\;. \qquad(6.13)\]

Ratios

If \(Z=Y/X\), then Equation 5.29 gives us \[ \frac{\,\mathrm{d}Z}{Z} = (\mu_y-\mu_x-\rho\sigma_x\sigma_y+\sigma_x^2)\,\mathrm{d}t + \sigma_y\,\mathrm{d}B_y - \sigma_x\,\mathrm{d}B_x\;. \qquad(6.14)\]

The instantaneous variance of \(\,\mathrm{d}Z/Z\) is therefore \[\begin{align*} \left(\frac{\,\mathrm{d}Z}{Z}\right)^2 &= (\sigma_y\,\mathrm{d}B_y - \sigma_x\,\mathrm{d}B_x)^2\\ &= (\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_y^2 - 2\rho\sigma_x\sigma_y)\,\mathrm{d}t\;. \end{align*}\] This implies:

Calculation Rule 6.5
The volatility of \(Y/X\) is
\[ \sqrt{\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_y^2 - 2\rho\sigma_x\sigma_y}\;. \qquad(6.15)\]

Theory Extra 6.1
To understand why taking the square root of \((\,\mathrm{d}Z/Z)^2\) (dropping the \(\,\mathrm{d}t\)) gives the volatility, consider for example the product case \(Z=XY\). Define a random process \(B\) by \(B_0=0\) and \[ \,\mathrm{d}B = \frac{\sigma_x}{\sigma}\,\mathrm{d}B_x + \frac{\sigma_y}{\sigma}\,\mathrm{d}B_y\;, \] {#eq-foreign}

where \(\sigma\) is the volatility defined in Equation 6.13. Then we can write Equation 6.12 as \[ \frac{\,\mathrm{d}Z}{Z} = \left(\mu_x +\mu_y+ \rho\sigma_x\sigma_y\right)\,\mathrm{d}t + \sigma\,\mathrm{d}B\;. \qquad(6.16)\]

From the discussion in Section 5.2, we know that \(B\) is a continuous martingale. We can compute its quadratic variation from \[\begin{align*} (\,\mathrm{d}B)^2 &= \left(\frac{\sigma_x\,\mathrm{d}B_x + \sigma_s\,\mathrm{d}B_s}{\sigma}\right)^2\\ &= \frac{(\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_s^2 + 2\rho\sigma_x\sigma_s)\,\mathrm{d}t}{\sigma^2}\; ,\\ &= \,\mathrm{d}t\;. \end{align*}\] By Levy’s theorem (see Section 4.3), any continuous martingale with this quadratic variation is necessarily a Brownian motion. Therefore, Equation 6.16 shows that \(\sigma\) is the volatility of \(Z\) as defined at the beginning of the section.
